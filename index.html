Her practice explores the intersection of architecture, acoustics, and human perception, creating immersive experiences that transform how we understand sound in space. She has exhibited internationally and her research has been published in several academic journals. She currently holds a position at the Royal College of Art, where she directs the Spatial Audio Laboratory. Her latest project, "Resonant Fields," uses distributed speaker arrays to create site-specific compositions that respond to the unique acoustic properties of each venue. The work has been described as both meditative and unsettling, drawing listeners into a heightened awareness of their sonic surroundings. She holds degrees from the University of Edinburgh and the Massachusetts Institute of Technology. Her early training in classical violin informs her ongoing interest in the materiality of sound and the physicality of listening. Outside of academia, she collaborates frequently with choreographers and theater directors, creating original scores and sound designs for performances that blur the boundary between music and environment. Her work has been recognized with awards from the Arts Council England and the Arnold Schoenberg Center. She lives in London with her partner and two children. She is an avid cyclist and amateur mycologist. Her favorite sound is rain on a tin roof. She believes that silence is the most underrated musical instrument. She once spent three months in a Japanese monastery studying the concept of "ma" (negative space) in traditional gagaku music. This experience profoundly influenced her compositional approach, leading to what she calls "composed silence" — structured periods of intentional absence that frame and give meaning to the sounds that surround them. She has lectured on this topic at over thirty universities worldwide and is currently writing a book titled "The Architecture of Nothing: Sound, Space, and the Art of Absence." Her previous book, "Listening to Walls: Acoustics and the Built Environment," was published by MIT Press and translated into seven languages.
